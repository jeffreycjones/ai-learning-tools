AI-Resilient Assignment Design Guide

Practical strategies for designing assessments that maintain academic integrity through structure, not surveillance. Build assignments where AI assistance is either irrelevant, insufficient, or transparent.

View Design Principles Jump to Strategies

Why design matters more than detection

AI detection tools produce probabilistic estimates, not definitive findings. False positives penalize honest students. False negatives reward dishonest ones. Detection accuracy degrades with each model generation, and students who want to evade detection can do so with minimal effort.

A more durable approach: design assignments where the structure itself makes AI-only completion either impossible, obvious, or beside the point. When an assignment requires personal experience, iterative process, live performance, or collaborative negotiation, AI becomes a tool rather than a replacement.

The goal is not to eliminate AI. It is to ensure that the assignment measures what you intend to measure, regardless of whether students use AI in the process. If AI can complete your assignment with no human judgment involved, the assignment is measuring the wrong thing.

Five principles of AI-resilient design

Apply these principles when creating or revising any assessment. An assignment does not need to satisfy all five, but the more it incorporates, the more resilient it becomes.

Principle 1

Require Personal Application

Connect the assignment to something only the student has access to: their internship, their campus organization, a local business they visited, their own career goals, or data they collected firsthand. AI cannot fabricate lived experience convincingly.

Principle 2

Make Process Visible

Require students to show their thinking at multiple stages: outlines, drafts, revision logs, annotated bibliographies, or reflection journals. When you can see the evolution of thought, AI-generated final products stand out because there is no trail behind them.

Principle 3

Include Live Performance

Add an in-class or synchronous component where students must defend, present, or apply their work in real time. A student who submits AI-generated analysis but cannot answer follow-up questions reveals the gap immediately.

Principle 4

Build in Iteration

Scaffold assignments across multiple submissions with feedback loops. Each revision should build on instructor or peer feedback from the prior stage. This creates a chain of evidence that is difficult to fabricate and easy to verify.

Principle 5

Require Collaboration or Negotiation

Design group tasks where the deliverable depends on real-time negotiation, role division, and synthesis of multiple perspectives. AI can draft a solo report, but it cannot navigate interpersonal dynamics, resolve disagreements, or integrate conflicting viewpoints in a team.

Strategies with before and after examples

Each strategy below shows a traditional assignment format (vulnerable to AI completion) alongside a redesigned version (resilient). These are drawn from common business school assignment types.

1. Personal Application

Anchor the assignment to a specific, verifiable personal context that AI cannot access.

Before (AI-Vulnerable) Traditional

"Write a 1,500-word analysis of a company's competitive strategy using Porter's Five Forces."

After (AI-Resilient) Redesigned

"Interview a manager at a company where you have interned or worked. Using that conversation and at least one publicly available data source, analyze the company's competitive position using Porter's Five Forces. Include direct quotes from your interview and explain where your interviewee's perspective confirms or contradicts what the framework predicts."

2. Process Documentation

Make the evolution of thinking part of the grade, not just the final output.

Before (AI-Vulnerable) Traditional

"Submit a final research paper on a topic in organizational behavior."

After (AI-Resilient) Redesigned

"Submit your paper in four stages: (1) a topic proposal with three candidate research questions and a rationale for each, (2) an annotated bibliography of 8 sources with a paragraph explaining how each connects to your question, (3) a rough draft with marginal comments explaining your reasoning in key sections, and (4) the final paper plus a 300-word reflection on how your argument changed from proposal to final draft. Each stage is graded, and later stages must reference instructor feedback from earlier ones."

3. Live Performance

Add a synchronous component where students must demonstrate understanding in real time.

Before (AI-Vulnerable) Traditional

"Submit a written case analysis of the Southwest Airlines case."

After (AI-Resilient) Redesigned

"Submit a two-page executive briefing on the Southwest Airlines case. During the following class session, you will deliver a 5-minute presentation of your analysis and respond to 3-4 follow-up questions from the instructor and peers. The presentation and Q&A are worth 40% of the assignment grade. You may use notes but not read from a script."

4. Iterative Feedback Loops

Create a chain of evidence where each stage builds on the last and references prior feedback.

Before (AI-Vulnerable) Traditional

"Create a marketing plan for a new product launch."

After (AI-Resilient) Redesigned

"Develop a marketing plan across three iterations. Version 1: submit a market analysis and positioning statement. You will receive written peer feedback from two classmates plus instructor comments. Version 2: revise your positioning based on feedback and add your channel strategy, including a memo explaining which feedback you incorporated and why. Version 3: final plan with an executive summary and a one-page appendix documenting your decision trail from Version 1 through Version 3."

5. Collaborative Negotiation

Design tasks where the deliverable requires real-time coordination between people.

Before (AI-Vulnerable) Traditional

"Write a group report analyzing the ethical implications of AI in hiring."

After (AI-Resilient) Redesigned

"Your team of four will conduct a structured debate on AI in hiring. Two members argue for expanded use; two argue against. After the debate (held in class), the full team collaborates on a joint policy recommendation that synthesizes both positions. Submit the recommendation plus individual reflections (300 words each) on how your thinking changed during the debate and what your partner's strongest argument was."

6. AI as an Explicit Tool (Transparency)

When AI use is appropriate, make it visible and gradeable rather than something to detect.

Before (AI-Vulnerable) Traditional

"Write a financial analysis memo for a prospective acquisition."

After (AI-Resilient) Redesigned

"Use an AI tool to generate a draft financial analysis memo for the acquisition case. Submit both the AI output and your revised version. In a 500-word critique, identify at least three errors, gaps, or oversimplifications in the AI draft. Explain how your revision improves on each. Your grade is based on the quality of your critique and revision, not the AI draft."

The AI use spectrum

Not every assignment needs the same level of restriction. Match your AI policy to the purpose of the assessment. The table below provides guidance on when each level is appropriate.

AI Level When to Use Assignment Examples What to Require
Prohibited The learning outcome depends on the student performing the cognitive work unaided. AI would bypass the skill being assessed. In-class essays, quantitative problem sets (show work), foreign language composition, competency exams Clear syllabus statement (Tier 1). In-class or proctored setting when feasible. Oral follow-up for high-stakes work.
Permitted with Disclosure AI assistance is realistic in practice, but you need to know what role it played in order to evaluate the student's contribution. Case analyses, research papers, presentations, project deliverables AI Use Statement with each submission. Grade based on both output quality and evidence of student judgment.
Required / Encouraged AI fluency is a course outcome. Students are expected to use AI and demonstrate skill in prompting, evaluating, and integrating AI output. Strategy consulting projects, marketing plans, data analysis, capstone deliverables Submit both AI output and human revision. Grade the critique, revision quality, and prompt sophistication.

Assignment self-check

Run any existing or new assignment through these seven questions. The more you can answer "yes," the more resilient the assignment is. Click each checkbox as you review.

Personal context: Does this assignment require students to draw on personal experience, local data, or a specific situation only they have access to?

Visible process: Do students submit intermediate work (drafts, outlines, annotations) that shows how their thinking developed?

Live component: Is there a synchronous moment (presentation, defense, in-class application) where students must demonstrate understanding in real time?

Feedback integration: Does a later stage of the assignment require students to respond to specific feedback from a prior stage?

Collaboration: Does the assignment involve real-time teamwork, negotiation, or synthesis of multiple perspectives that cannot be completed solo?

AI transparency: If AI use is permitted, does the assignment require students to show the AI output alongside their own revision and critique?

Outcome alignment: Does this assignment measure the learning objective it claims to? If AI can produce a passing submission with zero human judgment, the assignment is measuring AI capability, not student learning.

Scoring guide: 0-2 checks = high vulnerability (consider redesigning before next offering). 3-4 checks = moderate resilience (adequate for most contexts). 5-7 checks = strong resilience (well-designed for an AI-enabled environment).

Getting started

You do not need to redesign every assignment at once. Start with the one that matters most: your highest-stakes assessment, or the one where you suspect AI completion is most common. Apply one or two principles from this guide, pilot the revised version in a single section, and evaluate the results before scaling.
