AI Prompt Library for Course Design

Ready-to-use prompts for common course design tasks. Copy any prompt, customize the bracketed variables, and paste into TAMU AI Chat at tamu.ai.

How to use this library

This resource collects practical, ready-to-copy prompts for course design work that the 16 interactive tools don't cover. Each prompt is designed to be pasted directly into TAMU AI Chat with minimal customization.

To use a prompt:

  1. Find the prompt that matches your current task
  2. Click the Copy button to copy the full prompt
  3. Replace all [bracketed variables] with your own course details and context
  4. Paste into TAMU AI Chat and press enter
Note: These prompts are static reference guides. Unlike the interactive tools on the AI Tools page, you browse and copy here rather than filling out forms.

Lesson Planning & Module Design

Use these prompts to structure learning experiences from scratch, redesign existing sessions, or scaffold major assignments into manageable pieces.

Weekly Module Outline

When to use: Starting a new module from scratch

I am designing a [undergraduate/graduate] course in [subject] at a business school. Create a detailed outline for a one-week module on [topic]. Include: learning objectives (Bloom's Taxonomy verbs), a list of readings or resources to assign, 2-3 active learning activities, one formative assessment, and estimated time allocations for each component. The course is delivered in [8-week online / 16-week face-to-face / hybrid] format.

Flipped Classroom Activity Plan

When to use: Converting a lecture-heavy session to active learning

I currently deliver a 75-minute lecture on [topic] in my [course name] course. Help me redesign this as a flipped classroom session. Provide: a 10-15 minute pre-class video outline covering the essential content, 2-3 in-class activities that apply the concepts (with timing), a brief formative check at the end, and instructions I can give students for the pre-class preparation.

Scaffolded Assignment Sequence

When to use: Breaking a major project into staged deliverables

I assign a [final paper / capstone project / case analysis] in my [course name] course. Design a scaffolded sequence of 4-5 smaller deliverables that build toward the final submission. For each stage, provide: what students submit, the purpose of that stage, how it connects to the next, and brief grading criteria. The final project is due in Week [X] of an [8/16]-week term.

Rubric & Assessment Design

Use these prompts to create detailed rubrics, set up peer review, or plan balanced exams that assess the right level of thinking.

Analytic Rubric Builder

When to use: Creating a detailed rubric for any assignment

Create an analytic rubric for a [assignment type] in a [course level] [subject] course. The assignment asks students to [brief description]. Build a rubric with 4-5 criteria, each with 4 performance levels (Exemplary, Proficient, Developing, Beginning). Include point values totaling [X] points. Write descriptors that are specific and observable, not vague.

Peer Review Criteria

When to use: Setting up structured peer feedback activities

Design a peer review form for a [assignment type] in my [course name] course. Include 5-6 specific criteria students should evaluate, a rating scale (1-4) with descriptors for each level, and 2-3 open-ended questions that prompt constructive feedback. Keep language accessible for [undergraduate/graduate] students.

Exam Blueprint

When to use: Planning a balanced exam before writing questions

Create an exam blueprint for a [midterm/final] in [course name] covering [topics/chapters]. Map questions to learning objectives and Bloom's levels. Recommend a mix of question types (multiple choice, short answer, application problems) with point distributions. The exam should take [X] minutes and total [X] points. Weight higher-order thinking at [X]% of total points.

Student Communication & Engagement

Use these prompts to write warm course announcements, recurring check-ins, difficult feedback emails, and reflection prompts that gather meaningful student input.

Welcome Announcement

When to use: Writing the first course announcement before term starts

Write a warm, professional welcome announcement for [course name] at Mays Business School. Include: a brief introduction of myself as [your role/title], what students will learn, how the course is structured, 2-3 immediate action items for the first week, and where to find help. Tone should be approachable but professional. Keep it under 300 words.

Weekly Check-In Message

When to use: Drafting recurring weekly communication to students

Write a weekly check-in announcement for Week [X] of [course name]. This week covers [topic]. Include: a brief recap of what was accomplished last week, what is due this week (with dates), one tip for success on the current material, and a reminder of office hours or support resources. Keep it concise and scannable.

Difficult Conversation Email

When to use: Addressing performance concerns with a student

Draft a professional, empathetic email to a student who [is falling behind on assignments / received a low exam score / has not been participating]. Express concern without judgment, reference specific observations, offer concrete next steps or resources, and invite them to meet. Follow FERPA guidelines - do not include specific grades in the email subject line.

End-of-Course Reflection Prompt

When to use: Gathering meaningful student feedback beyond standard evaluations

Create a short end-of-course reflection for [course name] with 4-5 questions that help students articulate what they learned, how their thinking changed, what skills they developed, and how they might apply this in future coursework or careers. Frame questions to encourage honest, thoughtful responses rather than surface-level answers.

Course Content & Materials

Use these prompts to prepare students for dense readings, lead engaging case discussions, illustrate abstract concepts with real examples, and build reference glossaries.

Reading Guide / Study Guide

When to use: Helping students navigate dense readings

Create a reading guide for [article/chapter title] by [author] assigned in [course name]. Include: 3-4 key questions students should answer while reading, a list of key terms with brief definitions, 2-3 discussion-starter questions connecting the reading to course themes, and a brief note on why this reading matters for the course.

Case Discussion Facilitation Plan

When to use: Preparing to lead a case discussion in class

I am teaching [case name] in my [course name] class. Create a facilitation plan for a [50/75]-minute case discussion. Include: an opening question to launch discussion, 4-5 sequenced discussion questions that move from analysis to evaluation to recommendation, anticipated student responses and follow-up probes, a plan for managing dominant/quiet participants, and a 5-minute closing synthesis.

Real-World Application Example

When to use: Making abstract concepts concrete

Provide 3 real-world business examples that illustrate [concept] for [undergraduate/graduate] students in a [subject] course. For each example, include: the company or situation, what happened, how it connects to the concept, and one discussion question. Use examples from the last 5 years when possible.

Glossary of Key Terms

When to use: Building a course reference document

Create a glossary of 15-20 key terms for a [course name] course covering [topics]. For each term, provide: a concise definition (1-2 sentences), an example of how it applies in a business context, and any common misconceptions students have about the term. Format as a table.

Grading & Feedback

Use these prompts to write consistent, constructive feedback on individual student work, build reusable feedback banks for recurring issues, and document grading rationale.

Assignment Feedback Template

When to use: Writing consistent, constructive feedback on student work

I am grading a [assignment type] in [course name]. The student's submission [brief description of quality/issues]. Write constructive feedback that: acknowledges what the student did well, identifies 2-3 specific areas for improvement with concrete suggestions, connects feedback to the rubric criteria, and ends with an encouraging forward-looking statement. Keep feedback under 200 words.

Common Feedback Bank

When to use: Building reusable feedback comments for recurring issues

Create a bank of 10-12 reusable feedback comments for common issues in [assignment type] submissions in a [course name] course. Organize by category (e.g., argument quality, evidence use, formatting, critical thinking). Each comment should be specific enough to be useful but general enough to apply across students. Include both the issue and a constructive suggestion.

Grade Justification Narrative

When to use: Documenting grading rationale for borderline or contested grades

Help me write a brief, professional grade justification for a student who earned a [grade] on [assignment]. The main factors were [list 2-3 factors]. Write a 3-4 sentence narrative that references specific rubric criteria, is factual and objective, and could be shared with the student or used in a grade appeal process.

Course Improvement & Reflection

Use these prompts to gather mid-semester feedback, analyze what worked and what didn't, and audit your learning outcomes against actual assessments.

Mid-Semester Check-In Survey

When to use: Gathering actionable feedback at the midpoint

Design a 5-question mid-semester check-in survey for [course name]. Questions should surface actionable feedback about: pacing, clarity of expectations, effectiveness of activities, workload balance, and one open-ended improvement suggestion. Keep questions neutral and non-leading. Format as a list I can paste into a Canvas survey.

Course Redesign Analysis

When to use: Evaluating what to change for next semester

I just finished teaching [course name]. Based on these observations: [list 3-4 observations, e.g., 'students struggled with the group project,' 'exam 2 scores were low,' 'discussion participation dropped after week 4']. Analyze potential root causes for each issue and recommend specific, practical changes I could make for the next offering. Prioritize changes by likely impact.

Learning Outcome Alignment Audit

When to use: Checking that assessments match stated objectives

Here are my course learning objectives for [course name]: [paste objectives]. And here are my major assessments: [paste assessment list]. Audit the alignment between objectives and assessments. Identify any objectives that lack a direct assessment, any assessments that don't clearly map to an objective, and recommend adjustments to close gaps.
