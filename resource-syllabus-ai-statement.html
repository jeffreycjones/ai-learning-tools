How to use this template

Each tier below reflects a distinct instructional stance on AI use. Use the decision tree to identify the right tier for your course, then copy the corresponding statement into your syllabus alongside your academic integrity policy. Customize the bracketed placeholders to match your course.

At a glance: Tier 1 is for courses where AI would undermine core learning. Tier 2 is the recommended default for most Mays courses. Tier 3 is for courses that prepare students for professional contexts where AI fluency is an expected competency.

Decision tree: which tier fits your course?

Work through the questions in order. Stop at the first yes.

1. Do your course outcomes depend on unaided cognitive work?

If students must demonstrate a skill in their own voice (writing, analytical reasoning, quantitative work shown step by step, foreign language production, competency-based performance), AI assistance would undermine the learning. Choose Tier 1.

2. Would AI assistance change the work in ways relevant to grading?

If AI would affect quality, polish, or synthesis in ways you need to evaluate, you need transparency. Tier 2 lets students use AI but requires disclosure so you can grade both process and outcome.

3. Does the course prepare students for AI-fluent professional work?

If the target profession expects employees to use AI fluently (strategic analysis, marketing, data interpretation, consulting, product management), classroom practice should align with that reality. Choose Tier 3.

If in doubt, choose Tier 2.

It is the most flexible, places the lowest administrative burden on the instructor, and still teaches students to disclose AI use responsibly.

Tier 1: AI Prohibited

When to use: Courses whose outcomes depend on students demonstrating unaided cognitive work. Examples: first-year writing, quantitative reasoning courses where process matters, foreign language courses, courses with competency exams.

Statement to paste into your syllabus

AI Use Policy (Prohibited)

This course is designed to build [specific skill — e.g., analytical writing, quantitative reasoning, foundational vocabulary in the discipline]. Use of generative AI tools (including ChatGPT, Claude, Microsoft Copilot, Gemini, Grammarly's generative features, and similar platforms) to produce any portion of submitted work is prohibited unless explicitly approved by the instructor for a specific assignment.

Work determined to be AI-generated, in whole or in part, without approval will be treated as a violation of Texas A&M's Aggie Code of Honor and reported through the Aggie Honor System. Detection tools, writing fluency patterns, and instructor judgment may all inform this determination.

If you are unsure whether a tool qualifies as generative AI, ask before using it. Standard grammar and spell-check tools that do not include generative writing assistance remain permitted.

Tier 2: Permitted with Disclosure

When to use: Most business courses where AI assistance is realistic, expected, or welcome in professional practice but where transparency matters for grading, learning, and academic integrity. Recommended default for most Mays courses.

Statement to paste into your syllabus

AI Use Policy (Permitted with Disclosure)

Generative AI tools (including ChatGPT, Claude, Microsoft Copilot, Gemini, and similar platforms) may be used to support your work in this course, provided that you disclose how you used them.

For any assignment where you use AI, include a brief AI Use Statement at the end of your submission that identifies:

  1. Which tool or tools you used
  2. What you used them for (brainstorming, outlining, editing, drafting, analysis)
  3. What portion of the final work is AI-generated versus authored by you

You remain fully responsible for the accuracy, originality, and quality of all work you submit, regardless of AI involvement. AI outputs frequently contain errors, fabricated sources, and biased framings. Verify before you submit.

Assignment-level policies may tighten or loosen these expectations. When in doubt, check the assignment description or ask your instructor.

Tier 3: AI Encouraged

When to use: Courses that prepare students for professional work where AI fluency is a job-relevant skill. Examples: strategic analysis, marketing, data analytics, consulting capstones, and applied business courses where students are expected to produce work at professional velocity.

Statement to paste into your syllabus

AI Use Policy (Encouraged)

Fluent, responsible use of generative AI is a professional skill expected in the fields this course prepares you for. You are encouraged to use AI tools (including ChatGPT, Claude, Microsoft Copilot, Gemini, and similar platforms) throughout your work in this course, including for research, drafting, analysis, and refinement.

What is required of you:

  1. Disclose the AI tools you used and how you used them in an AI Use Statement accompanying each graded submission.
  2. Verify the accuracy of any claim, figure, or source produced with AI assistance. You are responsible for errors regardless of their origin.
  3. Demonstrate your own judgment in how you prompt, edit, and integrate AI output. The assignment is evaluated on both the final product and the quality of decisions made in producing it.

Use of AI does not reduce your responsibility for academic integrity, FERPA compliance, or the Aggie Code of Honor. Do not input confidential company data, classmates' work, or protected personal information into AI tools.

Citation examples for AI-generated content

When AI contributes substantively to a piece of work, cite it. These formats align with APA 7th and Chicago 17th conventions as of the publication date of this template.

APA 7th edition

In-text citation (OpenAI, 2026)  or  (Anthropic, 2026)
Reference list entry OpenAI. (2026). ChatGPT (GPT-4, April 15 version) [Large language model]. https://chat.openai.com
Reference list entry Anthropic. (2026). Claude (Sonnet 4.6) [Large language model]. https://claude.ai

APA recommends describing your prompt and the AI's output in the body of the paper when AI output is substantive to the argument.

Chicago 17th edition (Notes-Bibliography)

Footnote 1. Text generated by ChatGPT, OpenAI, April 15, 2026, https://chat.openai.com.
Bibliography OpenAI. ChatGPT. GPT-4, April 15 version. Accessed April 15, 2026. https://chat.openai.com.
